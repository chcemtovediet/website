<div class="student-dialog en">
It doesn't make sense if we want to give books to children, because we'd have to tear one apart. It makes sense if I want to read 5 books in four days and I'm asking how many books I need to read per day. Reading one and a quarter books per day makes sense.
</div>
<div class="teacher-dialog en">
Can you think of five other objects where using a quarter makes sense?
</div>
<div class="student-dialog en">
For example, five apples, because we can easily cut one apple into four pieces, while in the case of distributing books we don't want to destroy a book by dividing it into four parts.
</div>
<div class="student-dialog en">
We can divide books so that three get one book each and the fourth gets two.
</div>
<div class="teacher-dialog en">
Such division is possible, but those three will get a different fraction of five books than the fourth one. Fractions assume division into equal parts. A half is created when we divide a whole into two equal parts, a third when we divide a whole into three equal parts, a quarter by dividing into four equal parts, etc. The number of parts we divide the whole into determines the size of the fraction of that whole.
</div>
<div class="teacher-dialog en">

</div>
<div class="student-dialog en">

<div class="student-dialog en">
Because a quarter of students in one class has a different size than a quarter of students in the whole school.
</div>
<div class="student-dialog en">
And half of a small pizza has a different size than half of a large pizza.
</div>
<div class="teacher-dialog en">
Excellent example!
</div>
<div class="teacher-dialog en">
So we've found that fractions serve to divide something (which can be multiple objects, in mathematics we call this a set of objects) into equal parts. Then we can do something with one or several of these parts.
</div>
<div class="teacher-dialog en">
Imagine a family that buys a pizza, and mom and dad eat twice as much as two small children. How would you divide the pizza and what fraction would each of them eat?
</div>
<div class="student-dialog en">

</div>
<div class="teacher-dialog en">
We can imagine that what one child eats is one piece of pizza. Then each parent eats two pieces of pizza. Now we count the number of pieces we need to divide the pizza into: 1+1+2+2=6
</div>
<div class="teacher-dialog en">
How many pieces we divide the whole into, in our case six, we remember in the bottom part of the fraction, and how many such parts someone gets we remember in the top part of the fraction.
</div>
<div class="student-dialog en">
I know now, each child gets 1/6 and each parent gets 2/6.

...

<div class="teacher-dialog en">
